Digital currency regroup 🪖
Digital Currency Group (DCG) chief executive Barry Silbert has quit the board of crypto asset manager Grayscale. © 2019 Bloomberg Finance LP
Bazinga! Barry Silbert, the chief executive of the sprawling Digital Currency Group (DCG) crypto empire, has resigned from the board of DCG's crypto asset manager Grayscale as the conglomerate faces legal battles on multiple fronts.
- DCG was sued this year by New York attorney general Letitia James earlier this year, alleging the company and its subsidiary Genesis concealed $1.1 billion in losses from investors.
- DCG has also been sued by Gemini, the Winklevoss' crypto exchange, over their shared Earn crypto lending program, with Gemini seeking "damages and losses" from DCG's alleged "fraud and deception."

Across the board: Silbert, who chaired Grayscale's board, will be replaced by DCG's chief financial officer Mark Shifke, while DCG's vice president of operations, Matt Kummell, and Grayscale's chief financial officer, Edward McGee, were given board seats alongside Michael Sonnenshein, Grayscale's chief executive.
- DCG's president, Mark Murphy, also resigned from the board.

Cliff hanger: "Aligned with Grayscale’s commitment to responsible growth, we are pleased to welcome Mark Shifke, Matt Kummell and Edward McGee to Grayscale’s board of directors," a Grayscale spokesperson told Coindesk . "Grayscale and our investors will benefit from their respective experiences in the financial services and asset management industries as we prepare for Grayscale’s next chapter."

Zoom out: Silbert's resignation from the board comes as the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) is expected to imminently issue a decision on a slate of bitcoin spot exchange-traded fund (ETF) applications, including whether Grayscale can convert its flagship bitcoin trust to a fully-fledged bitcoin spot ETF.

Spot the difference: This week, Grayscale gave in to SEC demands that its converted bitcoin trust would be a cash creation and redemption ETF, rather than it preferred in-kind redemption mechanism, following BlackRock and other Wall Street giants jostling for pole position.
- "Grayscale finally surrendering to cash-only creations, was a big holdout. Pretty sure they have an AP agreement (a crucial last step) so that would check all the boxes. That said, still a mystery whether they will be allowed to go on day one of the Cointucky Derby," Bloomberg Intelligence analyst Eric Balchunas posted to X.
- There are rumors and "whispers" that Grayscale could challenge the "cash create hardline from SEC" in court.

Improving odds: "This is a decision from Barry to improve the odds of the ETF approval," Lumida Wealth Management chief executive Ram Ahluwalia posted to X. "Barry's resignation gives Grayscale greater autonomy."

Mt. Gox double spends 🗻
💸 Payments via PayPal to the creditors of the long-collapsed Mt. Gox have begun, according to social media reports, almost a decade after the bitcoin exchange imploded, losing users 850,000 bitcoin now valued at $36 billion. However, some recipients have claimed to have received their payouts twice, with the trustee asking for the second payment to be returned.
- Fears of the sudden deluge of returned bitcoin being sold off, a potential crypto market "black swan" event, were played down by UBS analysts earlier this year .

📩 "Due to a system issue, the transfer of money to you was inadvertently made twice," Mt. Gox's rehabilitation trustee Nobuaki Kobayashi wrote in an email to a number of creditors, posted to Reddit. "Please note that you are not authorized to receive the second transfer and are legally obligated to return the above amount to the rehabilitation trustee."

💱 Mt. Gox, at the time by far the largest bitcoin exchange, collapsed in early 2014 following a string of hacks that set the tone for crypto exchanges for the next ten years. The company's bankruptcy management was able to recover around 20% of the stolen funds.
